KL Divergence

The Kullback-Leibler Divergence (KL Divergence), is a measure from information theory that quantifies how one probability distribution (Q) differs from a true or reference probability distribution (P).

Think of it as "how much extra information is needed to describe the data if you use distribution Q to approximate distribution P." A higher KL divergence means that Q is a poorer approximation of P, leading to more "surprise" or "information loss."

Example: Comparing Customer Preferences for Movie Genres

Imagine a streaming service is analyzing customer preferences for three movie genres: Action, Comedy, and Drama.

Scenario: We have a "true" or ideal distribution of preferences we'd like to see from our users (e.g., from a market research survey, or a target demographic). Let's call this Ideal Distribution (P).

P (Ideal Customer Preferences):

  • Action: 40% (0.4)
  • Comedy: 30% (0.3)
  • Drama: 30% (0.3)

Now, we observe the actual preferences of a new segment of users (Segment X). Let's call this Observed Distribution (Q).

Q (Segment X Preferences):

  • Action: 50% (0.5)
  • Comedy: 40% (0.4)
  • Drama: 10% (0.1)

Interpretation:

The KL Divergence of approximately 0.1538 tells us that there's a measurable difference between the Ideal customer preferences (P) and the Observed preferences of Segment X (Q).

What contributed most to the divergence? The largest positive contribution came from the "Drama" genre (0.3294). This is because the Ideal distribution (P) expected 30% for Drama, but Segment X (Q) only showed 10%. This means Q significantly underestimates the probability of Drama relative to P, leading to a larger "surprise" or "information loss" if you use Q to describe P. The negative contributions from Action and Comedy mean that Q overestimates these probabilities compared to P, which reduces the overall divergence. In this scenario, a streaming service might use this information to:

Target Segment X with more Drama content if they want to nudge them towards the "ideal" preference. Understand that Segment X is less interested in Drama than the general ideal, and tailor recommendations accordingly. KL Divergence provides a single, quantitative measure of how much one probability distribution diverges from another, making it a powerful tool in various data analysis and machine learning tasks.

2. Define the utility function - KL Divergence

In[16]:
def calculate_kl_divergence(p_dist, q_dist):
    """
    Calculates the KL Divergence D_KL(P || Q).

    Args:
        p_dist (array-like): The "true" or reference probability distribution P.
                              Must sum to 1.
        q_dist (array-like): The "approximating" or comparison probability distribution Q.
                              Must sum to 1.

    Returns:
        float: The KL Divergence D_KL(P || Q). Returns np.inf if Q has zero
               probability where P has non-zero probability.
    """
    p_dist = np.asarray(p_dist, dtype=float)
    q_dist = np.asarray(q_dist, dtype=float)

    # Ensure probabilities sum to 1 (or close enough due to float precision)
    p_dist /= p_dist.sum()
    q_dist /= q_dist.sum()

    # Handle cases where Q(i) is 0 but P(i) > 0, which would lead to log(0)
    # entropy function from scipy.stats handles this by treating such terms as 0
    # when calculating log(P/Q) terms, effectively returning inf if P>0 and Q=0.
    return entropy(p_dist, q_dist)

# --- Calculate KL Divergence ---

kl_pq = calculate_kl_divergence(p_dist, q_dist)

Interpretation

Non-Zero Divergence: Both values are greater than 0. This immediately tells you that the two distributions (P for Control and Q for Treatment) are not identical. The new website design (Treatment group) did cause some shift in how users add items to their cart compared to the old design (Control group).

Magnitude of Divergence:

  • The values (0.02823 and 0.02853) are relatively small. This indicates that while there is a difference, the overall shape or pattern of the distribution of "items added to cart" did not undergo a massive transformation.
  • A KL divergence of 0 would mean the distributions are exactly the same. Values like 0.5, 1.0, or higher would indicate much more significant differences. In many practical scenarios, divergences below 0.1 are considered small.

Asymmetry:

  • Notice that D_KL(P || Q) (0.02823) is slightly different from D_KL(Q || P) (0.02853). This confirms the asymmetrical nature of KL Divergence. The "information loss" is marginally different depending on which distribution you consider the reference. In this specific case, the difference between P || Q and Q || P is very small, which is expected for distributions that are already quite similar.

What the Divergence Implies about Behavior:

  • To understand how the distributions differ, you'd look back at the actual probability values for each bin (e.g., "0 items," "1-2 items," etc.).
  • Recall from our previous example, the Control group had a higher proportion of "0 items" and lower proportions in higher item categories compared to the Treatment group. The KL Divergence quantifies this collective shift.
  • In practical terms: This small but non-zero divergence suggests that the new website design might have a subtle positive impact on user engagement related to adding items to the cart. While it's not a dramatic overhaul of behavior, it's a detectable shift.

What KL Divergence DOESN'T tell you (and why it's not a standalone A/B test metric):

  • Statistical Significance: KL Divergence itself doesn't directly provide a p-value or confidence interval to tell you if this observed difference is statistically significant (i.e., unlikely to have occurred by chance). For that, you'd still rely on traditional statistical tests (e.g., a chi-squared test for comparing categorical distributions, or a t-test if you were focusing on the mean number of items).
  • Direction of Change: While the number tells you how much they differ, you still need to look at the individual probability bins or a visualization (like the bar plot we generated) to understand in which direction the distribution shifted (e.g., more users adding 6+ items, fewer adding 0 items).

In conclusion:

A KL divergence of around 0.028 indicates that the new design (Treatment) did lead to a small, but discernible change in the overall pattern of how users add items to their cart compared to the old design (Control). This metric complements traditional A/B testing by giving you a single number to quantify the magnitude of this distributional shift.
